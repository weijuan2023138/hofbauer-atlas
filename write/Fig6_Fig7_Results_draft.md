## Figure 6. Hofbauer cell transcriptional reprogramming across pregnancy diseases.

To determine how pathological conditions reshape the Hofbauer cell landscape established through normal development (Figures 1–4) and the dual-track communication identity defined by TF programming (Figure 5), we compared Hofbauer cells from six clinical states: Normal_Early (first-trimester elective termination), Normal_Late (term, not in labor), Miscarriage (first-trimester recurrent miscarriage), Infection (first-trimester pathogen-exposed: *Toxoplasma gondii*, *Plasmodium falciparum*, *Listeria monocytogenes*), Preeclampsia (PE; late-gestation, five datasets), and Preterm birth (late-gestation spontaneous preterm, not in labor). UMAP visualization stratified by condition revealed that each disease state occupies a distinct region of transcriptional space, with infection and PE driving the most pronounced departures from their respective normal baselines (Figure 6a).

Quantification of subtype proportions exposed disease-specific compositional signatures (Figure 6b). In infection, the PRKN⁺ Autophagy subtype dominated the landscape, expanding to 71.4% of Hofbauer cells (versus 18.2% in Normal_Early), while the homeostatic and MHC-II⁺ populations contracted sharply. PE showed a contrasting pattern: the MHC-II⁺ Antigen-presenting subtype expanded from 28.9% (Normal_Late) to 41.3%, accompanied by a decline in the homeostatic compartment (35.7% to 18.6%). Preterm birth was characterized by Pro-inflammatory subtype expansion (7.3% to 26.8% versus Normal_Late), whereas miscarriage exhibited the most modest compositional shift, with a moderate increase in the vascular remodeling subtype.

Gene set enrichment analysis comparing each disease against its gestational-age-matched normal control revealed divergent pathway engagement (Figure 6c). Infection triggered the broadest transcriptional response, with strong activation of interferon-γ response (NES = 2.4), TNFα signaling via NF-κB (NES = 2.1), and inflammatory response pathways. PE activated complement cascades (NES = 1.8) and oxidative phosphorylation but suppressed MYC targets and E2F targets, consistent with reduced proliferative capacity. Preterm birth selectively activated IL6-JAK-STAT3 signaling and inflammatory response programs. Miscarriage showed minimal pathway-level perturbation, with only modest activation of apoptosis and p53 pathways.

At the single-gene level, a z-score dot plot of 25 representative genes across the six conditions crystallized these disease-specific signatures (Figure 6d). Infection was defined by massive upregulation of autophagy and lysosomal genes (*PRKN*, *SQSTM1*, *LAMP2*, *CTSD*) and innate immune effectors (*IL1B*, *CXCL8*, *CCL3*). PE featured elevated complement components (*C1QA*, *C1QB*, *C1QC*), MHC class II genes (*HLA-DRA*, *HLA-DPA1*), and the "don't eat me" signal *CD47*, alongside suppression of tissue remodeling genes (*SPP1*, *FN1*, *MMP9*). Preterm birth selectively upregulated *TNF*, *IL1B*, and *CXCL8*. Miscarriage cells showed isolated upregulation of *SPP1* and pregnancy-specific glycoproteins (*PSG3*, *PSG4*, *PSG5*), with minimal changes in immune effector genes.

To identify the upstream regulators driving these transcriptional shifts, we examined four key transcription factors — STAT1, NFKB1, CEBPA, and JUN — across all six conditions (Figure 6e). STAT1 and NFKB1 were strongly induced in infection (3.2-fold and 2.7-fold versus Normal_Early, respectively; both P < 0.001), consistent with the dominant interferon and NF-κB pathway activation observed in GSEA. CEBPA, the early-gestation lineage-determining TF, declined in infection (0.72 to 0.41, P < 0.001) and collapsed to its lowest level in PE (0.26, P < 0.001 versus Normal_Late). JUN, encoding c-Jun, was selectively elevated in infection. In preterm birth, NFKB1 was moderately elevated (P < 0.01) while CEBPA was preserved relative to Normal_Late, suggesting that the inflammatory activation in preterm birth operates without loss of lineage identity. Eight additional TFs (FOS, ID2, IRF1, IRF8, KLF4, MAFB, RELB, STAT3) showed disease-specific patterns consistent with this framework (Figure S6b).

We next asked whether PE, which showed the most complex transcriptional phenotype, reflects a systematic deviation from the normal developmental trajectory. We directly compared early-gestation (GW4.5–10) and late-gestation (GW32–38) Hofbauer cells within the PE cohort (Figure 6f–h). UMAP analysis revealed that PE early- and late-gestation cells partially overlapped in transcriptional space, in contrast to the clear temporal separation observed in normal development (Figure 6f). Subtype proportions across the PE developmental axis showed blunted temporal dynamics: the Pro-inflammatory subtype declined only modestly from early to late PE (38.2% to 22.5%, versus 42.1% to 7.3% in normal development), and the homeostatic subtype failed to expand appropriately (Figure 6g). Direct differential expression between early- and late-gestation PE cells identified 1,247 DEGs, substantially fewer than the 2,841 DEGs in the normal early-versus-late comparison, with GSEA revealing attenuated developmental pathway transitions (Figure 6h, Figure S6g). These results indicate that PE does not simply impose a disease program on top of normal development but actively disrupts the developmental trajectory itself.

To determine whether PE severity modulates TF programming, we stratified PE Hofbauer cells into three groups — Control, early-onset PE (EOPE, <34 weeks), and late-onset PE (LOPE, ≥34 weeks) — and examined four key TFs (Figure 6i). CEBPA showed a severity-dependent decline from Control to EOPE to LOPE, while STAT1 exhibited the inverse pattern, rising most prominently in EOPE. JUN and IRF1 displayed EOPE-specific elevation. These findings suggest that PE severity is encoded in the magnitude of TF dysregulation, with EOPE representing the most extreme departure from the normal regulatory state. Additional TFs (FOS, IRF1, IRF8, KLF4, NFKB1, STAT1, STAT3) are shown in Figure S6f.

Cross-disease DEG overlap analysis identified 23 genes differentially expressed in three or more diseases (Figure S6a), representing a shared Hofbauer cell stress response. External validation in an independent PE dataset (GSE298602) confirmed the core PE DEG signature (Figure S6e). CellChat analysis of Hofbauer subtype communication under disease conditions revealed infection-driven global rewiring of the subtype interaction network, with the PRKN⁺ subtype emerging as the dominant signal receiver (Figure S6c–d).

Together, these results establish that four pregnancy diseases — infection, miscarriage, PE, and preterm birth — reshape the Hofbauer cell landscape through distinct transcriptional programs, with infection driving PRKN⁺ autophagy-dominant reprogramming, PE uncoupling developmental progression from lineage identity, and preterm birth selectively amplifying inflammatory programs without loss of progenitor TF expression.

## Figure 7. Disease-specific disruption of the dual-track regulatory model.

Figure 5 established the dual-track TF regulatory model in normal development: CEBPA downregulation de-represses ECM communication genes (SPP1, FN1), while NFKB1/STAT3 activation directly induces immune adhesion genes (PTPRM, CD44, CD47). Here we ask how this regulatory logic is disrupted in miscarriage, infection, PE, and preterm birth.

We first visualized the spatial deployment of the two regulatory modules across the 21,681-cell Hofbauer atlas. UMAP coloring by mean expression of ECM module genes (FN1, SPP1, COL1A1, MMP9) and immune module genes (IL1B, TNF, CXCL8, CD44, CD47) revealed partially overlapping but spatially distinct distributions (Figure 7a), confirming that the two functional programs are executed by different cellular subpopulations.

We then examined how the core TFs governing each track respond to disease. Stratifying the six clinical states by gestational window, we compared CEBPA and NFKB1 expression in early-gestation (Normal_Early, Miscarriage, Infection) and late-gestation (Normal_Late, PE, Preterm) groups (Figure 7b). In the early window, CEBPA declined progressively from Normal_Early (mean 0.72) to Miscarriage (0.58) to Infection (0.41; P < 0.001), while NFKB1 rose in the opposite direction (0.35 → 0.62 → 0.96; P < 0.001). In the late window, CEBPA was selectively suppressed in PE (0.26 versus Normal_Late 0.69; P < 0.001) but preserved in Preterm (0.64; P = 0.12). NFKB1 was elevated in Preterm (P < 0.01) but unchanged in PE relative to Normal_Late. The activation-track TFs STAT3, STAT1, and RELB showed expression patterns closely paralleling NFKB1 (Figure S7a), while IRF1 and KLF4, which control distinct immune gene subsets, exhibited disease-specific regulation (Figure 7b).

To resolve whether TF expression changes are uniform or subtype-specific, we computed mean CEBPA and NFKB1 expression across six subtypes and six disease states (Figure 7c). CEBPA suppression in PE was highly uneven: the MKI67⁺ Proliferating subtype retained only 23% of its normal CEBPA level, PRKN⁺ Autophagy retained 27%, while Vascular remodeling retained 90%. This pattern indicates that the de-repression signal is concentrated in subtypes that do not express ECM genes, potentially explaining why global CEBPA loss in PE fails to generate a corresponding ECM output. NFKB1 displayed a disease-selective activation pattern: in infection, all subtypes showed NFKB1 elevation (163–394% of Normal_Early), whereas in PE, only the Pro-inflammatory subtype exhibited notable upregulation (227%). IRF1 and KLF4 heatmaps revealed further disease-subtype specificity (Figure 7d–e; STAT1, STAT3: Figure S7b).

To understand why CEBPA and NFKB1 exhibit divergent disease behaviors, we assessed the coupling between TF mRNA expression and downstream regulon activity — defined as mean expression of known target genes from AnimalTFDB — across diseases (Figure 7h). NFKB1 showed genuine mRNA-activity coupling in Normal_Late (R² = 0.12) and Preterm (R² = 0.25), indicating that transcriptional changes in NFKB1 are transmitted to target gene programs. In stark contrast, CEBPA mRNA-activity coupling was near zero across all conditions (R² = 0.00–0.03), revealing that CEBPA functional output is dominated by post-transcriptional regulation. This fundamental difference explains a key paradox: CEBPA mRNA declines dramatically in PE, yet downstream ECM genes are not correspondingly de-repressed. The six-group slope analysis confirmed these patterns across all disease states (Figure S7c).

We next evaluated whether TF dysregulation propagates to downstream functional module output (Figure 7f). In infection, the immune module score rose sharply (median 0.62 versus Normal_Early −0.18; P < 0.001), consistent with global NFKB1/STAT3 activation. The ECM module showed a moderate increase (P < 0.01), reflecting concurrent de-repression. In preterm birth, the immune module was selectively elevated (P < 0.001) while the ECM module remained at Normal_Late levels, matching the preserved CEBPA and elevated NFKB1 pattern. In PE, despite profound CEBPA suppression, the ECM module did not increase (P = 0.34 versus Normal_Late), confirming that CEBPA loss in ECM-non-bearing subtypes fails to generate functional ECM output. More strikingly, the immune module in PE exhibited a split phenotype: CXCL8 declined 4.7-fold (P < 0.001), TNF declined 2.3-fold (P < 0.001), yet CD47 was selectively upregulated (1.3-fold, P < 0.001). This "intact upstream, fragmented downstream" pattern — NFKB1 expression maintained, but inflammatory effectors suppressed while immune evasion signals are activated — suggests a signaling blockade operating between NF-κB activation and its canonical transcriptional targets in PE. Miscarriage showed the most muted changes in both modules (Figure S7e).

To capture the global relationship between the ECM and immune tracks, we projected Hofbauer cells from each clinical state into ECM-Immune dual-dimensional space (Figure 7g). In early gestation, the 95% confidence ellipses for Normal_Early, Miscarriage, and Infection were highly overlapping, indicating that dual-track separation has not yet been established in the first trimester. In late gestation, Normal_Late cells occupied a central-right position reflecting established dual-track equilibrium. Preterm cells shifted rightward and upward (immune activation), consistent with premature triggering of the immune track. PE cells shifted leftward and downward, reflecting the dual-track decline — ECM output blunted by mislocalized CEBPA loss, immune output fragmented by the downstream signaling blockade. Infection was excluded from the late-gestation comparison as it is a first-trimester condition.

Finally, we dissected the PE split phenotype at the effector gene level (Figure 7i). CXCL8, TNF, IL1B, and CCL3 were all significantly suppressed in PE versus Normal_Late (0.22–0.46 fold; all P < 0.001), while CD47 and HLA-DRA were elevated. Preterm birth showed the opposite effector profile: CXCL8, TNF, and IL1B were all elevated, with CD47 unchanged. Infection drove maximal upregulation of all effector genes examined. This effector-level comparison decomposes the global dual-track shifts into specific functional programs: infection triggers broad-spectrum activation, preterm birth selectively amplifies inflammatory chemokines, and PE silences canonical inflammatory effectors while upregulating immune evasive signals.

Together, these results reveal that the same dual-track regulatory network is disrupted through four mechanistically distinct modes: infection triggers near-complete reprogramming with dual-track co-activation and PRKN⁺ autophagy dominance; preterm birth selectively pre-activates the immune track through NFKB1-driven transcriptional coupling while preserving CEBPA-mediated lineage identity; PE uncouples the two tracks through subtype-mislocalized CEBPA suppression, post-transcriptional CEBPA silencing, and a signaling blockade downstream of NFKB1 that selectively silences inflammatory effectors while permitting CD47-mediated immune evasion; and miscarriage produces the mildest disruption, consistent with tissue clearance rather than active pathology.
